As MTA cracks down on fare evasion, some West Harlem residents are skeptical

West Harlem commuters’ concerns surround equity in fare evasion enforcement application, as well as the feasibility of proposed strategies.

By Franka Etkin

Graphics by Emily Ta and Claire Jong

November 25, 2025

Marcus Bui / Columbia Daily Spectator

Amid new MTA tactics to enforce transit fares, some West Harlem commuters have expressed concerns about the impact of these strategies in their neighborhood.

A report published by the Citizens Budget Commission, a fiscally conservative watchdog organization, on Sept. 11 detailed an almost $1 billion loss in revenue in 2024 due to fare evasion. The report notes that there has been a decrease in fare evasion observed from the first quarter of 2024 to the first quarter of 2025, attributing this to a recent increase in fare evasion control tactics. If these trends continue, the MTA’s losses due to fare evasion will decrease by approximately $100 million.

Jai Patel, the chief financial officer of the MTA, announced the launch of a proof of payment strategy in an interview with Bloomberg Businessweek Daily in August. The system, which is meant to combat fare evasion, could be similar to “European-style proof of payment for customers. … You can tap their phones or credit cards or method of payment to see if they paid their fare,” Patel explained. It would be implemented once the subway system is fully transitioned from the MetroCard to exclusively OMNY, the new method of payment for the MTA.

In cities like Paris, unarmed inspectors can check Metro tickets at any time by tapping them against a reader, to see if it’s been used to pay for a ride.

Karen Anderson, a commuter in West Harlem who frequents the 125th Street station, shared her concerns about the feasibility of the proof of payment strategy. “Unfortunately, something like that, it’s gonna be kind of tough. And very, very expensive. I’m not sure it’s worth it,” she said.

Aside from proof of payment, the MTA has employed a variety of tactics in recent years to enforce transit fares. This year, the MTA installed fins and spiked sleeves on turnstiles as a part of its campaign against fare evasion. The MTA doubled its number of privately hired unarmed security guards in 2024, stationing them by subway entrances and exits. Evasion and Graffiti Lawlessness Eradication teams are also doing checks for payment on buses.

In May 2023, the Blue-Ribbon Panel on MTA Fare and Toll Evasion produced a report that highlighted the prominence of the fare evasion problem and proposed strategies that emphasized deterrence rather than an increase in criminalization and punishment.

Aya Tanaka, a West Harlem resident and commuter, said that while she understands the purpose of the fins and spikes, she is unsure of their effectiveness and that she finds them “too policing” and “nasty.”

Robert Burnson, another commuter, reflected this sentiment.

“I don’t like those. They need to get rid of those,” referring to turnstile spikes. “People could still go through. That’s a waste of time, waste of taxpayer’s money.”

According to New York Police Department data, Transit District 3—which includes stations in Morningside Heights, Hamilton Heights, Manhattanville, among others—has reported 4,893 summonses issued and 525 arrests related to fare evasion so far this year. By the same point in 2018, District 3 had only reported 1,856 summonses and 170 arrests related to fare evasion.

Harold Stolper, a lecturer of international and public affairs, has researched the disproportionate effects of the MTA’s fare evasion enforcement tactics as an economist for the Community Service Society of New York.

“If the idea is to stop people at random, that introduces the scope for police discretion and who they stop and where, it’s the potential for bias,” Stolper said, expressing concern about the proposed proof of payment system.

He also noted that fare evasion “has been a big issue for a while now.” As for why people avoid the fare, “there are a lot of people who have a hard time affording the subway and bus fare, and fare evasion is, at least a large part, due to its underlying economic need,” Stolper said.

Some commuters in West Harlem agree. When asked why she thought people evaded the fare, Anderson responded, “probably expense.”

Burnson alternatively suggested that he thinks people “don’t want to pay” because they don’t find the price worth the unreliability of the subway services. “They don’t give you no good service, always late, always have problems with the train, always crowded,” he said.

The MTA has been underfunded for years, creating perpetual problems with efficiency and outdated infrastructure.

Stolper’s research found that high poverty neighborhoods were disproportionately impacted by fare evasion enforcement tactics, and, more specifically, that high poverty neighborhoods with predominantly Black and Latino constituents were more affected than that of predominantly white and Asian ones.

In response to the Community Service Society’s research and advocacy, the MTA launched the Fair Fares NYC subsidy program in 2019. Since then, the program has provided a 50 percent subsidy on subways and buses for 376,096 New York City residents. In January, the program was expanded to include individuals up to 145 percent above the federal poverty level regardless of immigration status, a step up from its previous 120 percent of the FPL requirement. On Oct. 30, the threshold was pushed to 150 percent.

Yet with the MTA’s plans to raise subway fares from $2.90 to $3 in January, many advocates argue that this expansion is not enough. Awareness of the program remains low, with only around 33 percent of eligible riders enrolled, according to an estimate by CSS. Many argue for an expansion of the program to include households that make up to 200 percent of the FPL requirement, citing that such an expansion in eligibility would, in turn, decrease need-based fare evasion.

In City Council District 7, pushing the threshold to 200 percent would lead to an increase of approximately 7,500-10,000 commuters being eligible for the program, according to data published by the Permanent Citizens Advisory Committee to the MTA.

Professor Elliott Sclar, who specializes in transportation and urban planning at Columbia University’s Graduate School of Architecture, Planning and Preservation put the problem of subsidizing public transportation into a “bigger perspective,” in an interview with Spectator.

“Public transportation never really ever paid for itself at the fare box, it’s always required subsidy,” he said in reference to New York City’s first trolley car lines. “All transportation is subsidized, even people who drive cars don’t necessarily pay the full cost of the streets they use.”

The MTA website states that, “the largest share of MTA revenue—$7.222 billion—comes from dedicated taxes and subsidies the Authority receives from the cities and states that we serve.” The second highest contributor is fares, which account for 23 percent of the budget.

A key point in mayor-elect and former Morningside Heights resident Zohran Mamdani’s campaign is his promise to make New York City buses free. While its likelihood of implementation remains in question, Sclar says the proposal is not unprecedented.

“The notion of free buses has kicked around for years and years in transportation policy circles,” he said. Sclar went on to describe the dilemma between raising fares and promoting affordability as a “constant balancing act.”

“I’m an agnostic on this question of free buses,” he said. “This is the ongoing, larger policy question, because the MTA, their board, they have to continually close the gap between their costs and their revenues.”

Tanaka says she’d like to see buses reflect the policies of some New York City museums—being free for New York City residents as taxpayers, but paid for by tourists. “I would love that to be true for us, New Yorkers,” she said.

“I feel like the bus and the train should be free,” Jazmyn Weeks, BC ’27, said. “Upping the prices and claiming that we’re going to add more cops or whatever doesn’t help. It just further victimizes people, because if you can’t even pay for the bus and then you’re giving someone a $50 to $100 fine, what’s the point? They still can’t pay it.”

While the MTA’s future plans for proof of payment remain vague, the current strategies being offered raise concerns over equity.

“You can’t police away poverty and economic need,” Stolper said.
